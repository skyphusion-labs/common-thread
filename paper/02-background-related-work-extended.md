# 2. Background and related work

This section establishes the conceptual and empirical landscape against which the methodology is positioned. The first part (§2.1) defines the terms the methodology uses. The middle parts (§2.2 through §2.10) survey the prior work that bears on the methodology, organized by the layer of the problem each line of work addresses: automated-account detection, coordinated-behavior detection at the network level, authorship attribution, OSINT practitioner methodology, commercial and proprietary tooling, visual forensics, networked privacy and pseudonymity scholarship, threat modeling for vulnerable populations, and the legal and ethical context. The final part (§2.11) states what the methodology contributes that the surveyed prior work does not.

The section is intended to position the methodology accurately rather than exhaustively. Practitioners and researchers using the methodology should treat the citations here as entry points to the broader literatures rather than as comprehensive bibliographies of any of the fields surveyed. Full bibliographic details for the cited works appear in the References section at the end of the paper; specific publication details should be verified against the original sources before relying on them.

## 2.1 Definitions

The terminology in this domain is contested and inconsistent across the relevant literatures. The methodology adopts the following definitions and uses them consistently throughout the paper.

**Sockpuppet.** An account operated by a person other than the one the account presents itself as, or an account that is one of several operated by a single person who presents the accounts as independent. The term originates in online forum culture of the late 1990s and has been adopted with various refinements in the academic literature (Solorio et al., Bhattacharya and colleagues at Stony Brook, and the broader literature on coordinated inauthentic behavior). The methodology uses the term to refer to the structural condition (multiple accounts operated by a common hand and presented as independent) rather than to any specific intent.

**Persona.** A presented identity associated with an account. A single operator may maintain multiple personas across multiple accounts; the methodology investigates whether multiple personas share an operator.

**Alias.** A name used by an operator in place of another name. The methodology uses "alias" specifically for the relationship between an account's presented identity and the operator's other presented identities, not as a synonym for sockpuppet.

**Pseudonym.** A name used by an operator in place of their legal name. Pseudonyms are not inherently inauthentic. Many legitimate uses of pseudonymity are protected by professional, ethical, and legal norms, and the academic literature on networked privacy (boyd, Marwick, Nissenbaum; see §2.8) has established the conceptual framework for understanding pseudonymity as a legitimate privacy practice rather than as inauthenticity. The methodology is designed to distinguish coordinated inauthentic use of pseudonyms from legitimate pseudonymity (the discussion in §2.3 and §2.8 develops this distinction).

**Automated account or bot.** An account whose posting and engagement is performed by software, with limited or no human intervention at the per-post level. Bot detection is a separate problem from sockpuppet attribution. The methodology is designed for human-operated accounts and does not produce useful outputs against fully automated accounts.

**Coordinated inauthentic behavior (CIB).** The term, originated by Meta (then Facebook) in 2018 to describe networks of accounts working together to mislead about their identity or coordination, has been adopted across the platform and academic literatures. The methodology uses the term in this sense: networks of accounts working in coordination while presenting themselves as independent. Subsequent academic refinement (Pacheco and colleagues, the Stanford Internet Observatory's work on platform integrity, the Berkman Klein Center's work on platform governance) has produced more precise operational definitions; the methodology adopts the Meta-origin term while acknowledging that its boundaries are debated.

**Parallel operator.** One natural person operating multiple accounts. The methodology attributes accounts to parallel operators rather than to natural persons (§3.3.3 develops this distinction).

**Network attribution.** The act of determining that a set of accounts are operated by a common hand. The methodology produces network attribution at the cluster level, not at the natural-person level.

## 2.2 Automated-account detection

The detection of automated accounts on social platforms has substantial academic and applied literature. Cresci's 2020 survey article "A decade of social bot detection" in Communications of the ACM is the most comprehensive single source on the academic state of the art and is the recommended entry point. The survey synthesizes a decade of work from the Indiana University Observatory on Social Media, Cresci's own group at IIT-CNR in Pisa, and the broader community.

The Botometer service (originally introduced as BotOrNot by Davis, Varol, Ferrara, Flammini, and Menczer in 2016) is the most cited academic tooling in this space. Subsequent papers in the Botometer line refine the underlying classifiers (Yang et al. on scalable bot detection, Varol and colleagues on detection-estimation-characterization), address adversarial robustness (Cresci and colleagues on adversarial social bots), and extend the methodology to platforms beyond Twitter. Ferrara and colleagues' 2016 CACM article "The rise of social bots" is the canonical popular-academic framing of the problem.

Applied bot detection tooling outside the academic mainstream includes BotSentinel (founded by Bouzy, focused on Twitter, with a journalistic rather than academic methodology), DataReportal indicators, Truthnest, and various platform-internal bot-detection systems. The applied tooling differs from the academic tooling in transparency: applied tools typically do not publish their classifiers' weights or their training data, while academic tools more often do.

The Indiana University group has also published BotSlayer (Hui et al. 2019) for real-time detection of bot amplification, and the broader Observatory on Social Media maintains a suite of tools accessible to academic and journalist users. These tools are useful for the initial filtering step when a seed set may contain both automated and human-operated accounts.

The methodology distinguishes itself from this body of work by addressing the human-operated case rather than the automated case. The detection problems are structurally different: automated accounts produce signal patterns (millisecond response latencies, exact temporal regularities, classifier-detectable text generation) that are absent from human-operated accounts. The methodologies developed for bot detection do not transfer cleanly to sockpuppet attribution, and the literature on the bot detection problem does not directly inform the methodology's signal taxonomy except as a contrast.

The relationship between the two problems is complementary. Practitioners who suspect a network may contain both bots and human-operated accounts can apply bot detection first to remove the automated accounts from the seed set, then apply the methodology to the remaining accounts. The reference implementation supports this workflow but does not implement bot detection internally; practitioners should use established bot-detection tooling for that purpose.

## 2.3 Coordinated-behavior detection at the network level

A separate body of work addresses coordinated inauthentic behavior at the network level rather than at the individual-account level. The most directly relevant academic work is the Indiana University Observatory on Social Media (OSoMe) program, with Pacheco and colleagues' 2021 paper "Uncovering coordinated networks on social media: methods and case studies" providing the methodological framework most closely related to Common Thread. The OSoMe work typically operates over substantial portions of a platform's social graph and produces network-level claims (this set of accounts is coordinated) rather than pair-level attribution claims (these two accounts share an operator).

Nizzoli and colleagues have published applications of coordinated-behavior detection methodology to specific election contexts (2019 UK general election, 2020 US presidential election), demonstrating the methodology's application to high-stakes political settings. Their work is useful background for practitioners conducting election-related investigations.

The Atlantic Council's Digital Forensic Research Lab (DFRLab) has published numerous investigation reports applying network-level methodology to specific cases (state-sponsored influence operations, coordinated disinformation campaigns, election-period coordinated activity). The DFRLab methodology is closer to investigative-journalism practice than to academic methodology, with explicit case-by-case reasoning that the methodology paper here aims to systematize for accessibility. The DFRLab's online toolkit and methodology notes are recommended practitioner reading.

The Stanford Internet Observatory (SIO) under DiResta and others has produced substantial work on coordinated inauthentic behavior, including detailed analyses of state-sponsored networks (Internet Research Agency operations, Chinese state-coordinated activity, and various other cases). The SIO work combines technical analysis with policy-oriented framing and is recommended as a complement to the more methodology-focused academic literature.

Starbird's group at the University of Washington has published on disinformation campaigns and the role of crowdsourced participation in coordinated narratives. The framing of "participatory disinformation" developed in this work is useful for understanding how legitimate communities of belief can be folded into coordinated campaigns without all participants being aware of the coordination.

Graphika, a commercial firm, has produced substantial analytic work on coordinated inauthentic behavior, often in partnership with platforms or with academic researchers (the joint work with Stanford on Internet Research Agency operations is a notable example). The Graphika methodology is proprietary; their public reports describe findings but not in sufficient detail to support replication outside their tooling.

The methodology presented here differs from this body of work along three dimensions. First, it operates at the cluster level rather than at the platform-network level, allowing investigations that have only the bandwidth to focus on tens of accounts rather than thousands. Second, it does not require platform partnership or paid API access at the scale that network-level analysis typically demands. Third, it is published with explicit methodology and a reference implementation, making it accessible to practitioners outside the institutional contexts that the existing network-level work serves.

These differences are not improvements over the network-level work; they are different design points. Practitioners with platform partnership and the resources for network-level analysis should use the network-level tooling. Practitioners without those resources have been served less well by the existing tooling; the methodology aims to serve them.

## 2.4 Authorship attribution

Authorship attribution as a computational field predates social media by decades. Mosteller and Wallace's 1964 monograph "Inference and Disputed Authorship: The Federalist" is the canonical early demonstration that authorship can be inferred from statistical analysis of word frequencies; their analysis of the disputed Federalist Papers established the methodology that subsequent authorship attribution work has elaborated. Holmes's 1998 review "The evolution of stylometry in humanities scholarship" provides historical context for the field's development through the 1990s.

The field has developed substantially since, with successive surveys consolidating the state of the art at multiple points. Juola's 2008 monograph "Authorship attribution" in Foundations and Trends in Information Retrieval is the most comprehensive treatment of the field through the mid-2000s. Koppel, Schler, and Argamon's 2009 paper "Computational methods in authorship attribution" in JASIST surveys the methodological landscape with a particular focus on supervised learning approaches. Stamatatos's 2009 paper "A survey of modern authorship attribution methods," also in JASIST, is the most cited single survey article in the field.

Burrows' Delta, introduced in his 2002 paper "'Delta': A measure of stylistic difference and a guide to likely authorship" in Literary and Linguistic Computing (now Digital Scholarship in the Humanities), remains the foundational stylometric method and is the methodology's specified baseline for the linguistic signals at §4.3.1 and §6.2.3. The Delta method's strength is its simplicity and the stability of its results across implementations: an investigation that specifies Burrows' Delta and a specific function-word list produces reproducible distances across any compliant implementation. Subsequent work (Hoover's refinements, Eder's evaluation studies) has examined the method's properties and limitations.

The literature on stylometric defense demonstrates that motivated authors can defeat stylometric attribution by modifying their writing systematically. Brennan and Greenstadt's 2009 paper "Practical attacks against authorship recognition techniques" at the IAAI conference established the framework, and McDonald, Afroz, Caliskan-Islam, Stolerman, and Greenstadt's 2012 paper "Use fewer instances of the letter 'I': Toward writing style anonymization" developed it into the Anonymouth tool at Drexel. Afroz, Brennan, and Greenstadt's subsequent work on detecting stylometric deception (the inverse problem: identifying when a text has been deliberately obscured) is relevant background for the methodology's adversarial-context considerations. This work shapes the methodology's posture: §3.3.4 acknowledged that adversarially adapted operators are harder to detect, and the stylometric defense literature is the specific work that demonstrates this.

Large-scale internet-era authorship attribution has been explored in Narayanan and colleagues' 2012 IEEE Security and Privacy paper "On the feasibility of internet-scale author identification," which demonstrated that authorship attribution at the scale of millions of authors is technically feasible. This work raises both methodological possibilities and ethical concerns; the methodology's audience exclusions in §1.2 are partly informed by the implications of large-scale attribution capability.

The application of authorship attribution to social-media-length texts faces particular challenges that the field has addressed in mixed ways. Tweets and similar short-form posts provide much less text per author than the corpora most stylometric methods were developed against; the minimum corpus size requirements specified at §6.4.1 (typically several thousand words per author) reflect the practical lower bound. Below this bound, stylometric signals become unreliable, and the methodology's response is to flag insufficient-data cases rather than to produce attribution from inadequate input.

Adversarial scenarios where authors specifically intend to defeat attribution (the scenario the methodology faces with sophisticated operators) are not the typical evaluation setting for authorship attribution research. The academic literature evaluates methods against authors who have not adapted their writing. The methodology accepts that performance against adapted authors is below the published performance against unadapted authors and does not represent the published numbers as applicable to the methodology's adversarial setting.

## 2.5 OSINT practitioner methodology

Open-source intelligence (OSINT) practice has developed a substantial body of methodology that is largely community-maintained rather than academic. Bellingcat, an investigative-journalism organization founded by Higgins, has published an online investigator's guide that is widely regarded as the canonical practitioner reference for OSINT methodology applied to social-media investigations. The Bellingcat guide covers collection techniques, verification standards, ethical considerations, and case studies; it overlaps substantially with the methodology in scope but differs in its target output. Higgins's 2021 book "We Are Bellingcat" provides historical and operational context for the organization's methodology.

The Bellingcat guide is designed for investigators conducting one-off investigations of specific subjects, typically producing investigative reports rather than structured methodology outputs. The methodology presented here is designed for investigations that produce evidence packets suitable for use beyond the investigation itself (court filings, peer review, replication by other practitioners). The two approaches are complementary; practitioners conducting OSINT investigations should be familiar with both.

Various community-maintained resources extend or specialize the Bellingcat methodology. Bazzell's "Open Source Intelligence Techniques" (now in its tenth edition) is the canonical reference for OSINT techniques at the level of specific tools and methods. The OSINT Curious project, the IntelTechniques community, Trace Labs, and several others maintain practitioner resources at varying levels of formality. These resources address specific techniques (geolocation, archive preservation, social engineering, blockchain analysis) rather than the network-attribution problem the methodology focuses on.

Academic work on OSINT methodology is sparser but growing. Glassman and Kang's 2012 paper "Intelligence in the internet age: The emergence and evolution of Open Source Intelligence (OSINT)" provides an academic framing of the practice. Practitioner-academic crossover work has been published in venues such as the Journal of Strategic Security and the International Journal of Intelligence and Counterintelligence.

The methodology distinguishes itself from the OSINT practitioner literature by its emphasis on structured methodology with explicit confidence calibration and chain-of-custody discipline. OSINT practice tends toward case-specific judgment, which is appropriate for the journalistic and investigative-research contexts most OSINT work operates in. The methodology accepts that some of the flexibility OSINT practice offers is sacrificed in exchange for the reproducibility properties that court and peer-review contexts require.

## 2.6 Commercial and proprietary tooling

The commercial tooling landscape includes Graphika, the various enterprise products from Recorded Future, Flashpoint, ZeroFox, Maltego (graph-based OSINT), Palantir's various platforms, and similar firms, and the internal trust-and-safety tooling maintained by major platforms. These tools produce capability that exceeds the methodology's reach in several dimensions: scale of data ingestion, sophistication of analysis, dedicated research staff applying the tooling to specific cases, and integration with downstream operational systems.

The commercial tooling is inaccessible to the methodology's intended practitioner audience in two related ways. First, the licensing costs typically exceed what individual practitioners, small newsrooms, and most academic researchers can afford. Second, the methodologies are proprietary; even practitioners who can afford the tooling cannot publish work that depends on it in a form that supports external verification, because the methodology itself is not externally documented.

Major platforms maintain internal coordinated-behavior detection systems that operate over platform-internal data the methodology does not have access to. Public-facing documentation of these systems (Meta's threat-reports series, Twitter's transparency reports during the API-open period, Google's threat-analysis-group publications) describes findings without disclosing methodology in sufficient detail to support external evaluation. These systems are the most capable in the landscape but are also the least externally accountable: they operate without external visibility into their methodology, error rates, or decision processes. The methodology in §10.6 argues that the existence of these closed methodologies is itself part of the case for publishing an open methodology, because the open methodology brings external accountability to a domain where closed methodologies operate without it.

## 2.7 Image and visual forensics

The methodology's visual-signal category (§4.5) draws on a body of work in image forensics that is separate from the social-media-investigation literature.

Perceptual hashing, the basis for the methodology's profile-image comparison, has a literature dating to the late 1990s and consolidated in the 2000s. Zauner's 2010 thesis "Implementation and Benchmarking of Perceptual Image Hash Functions" at the University of Applied Sciences, Hagenberg, is the methodology's reference for the technique. The pHash and dHash hash families specified at §6.2.5 are standard in the literature and have widely available implementations. Farid's 2009 IEEE Signal Processing Magazine article "Image forgery detection" provides a broader survey of image forensic techniques beyond perceptual hashing, and is recommended as background for practitioners working with visual signals.

GAN-generated image detection is a more recent and rapidly evolving field. Marra, Gragnaniello, Cozzolino, and Verdoliva's 2018 paper "Detection of GAN-generated fake images over social networks" was an early contribution to this literature. Wang, Wang, Zhang, Owens, and Efros's 2020 CVPR paper "CNN-generated images are surprisingly easy to spot... for now" is the most cited single paper on the detection problem and was the basis for several subsequent detector implementations. Rossler and colleagues' 2019 paper "FaceForensics++: Learning to detect manipulated facial images" extends detection to face-manipulation contexts beyond pure GAN synthesis. Verdoliva's 2020 IEEE Journal of Selected Topics in Signal Processing survey "Media forensics and deepfakes: an overview" provides field-state context.

The arms-race dynamics in this field are well-documented. Practitioners using GAN detection should be aware that detector performance against the latest generation of synthesis models lags the synthesis models themselves. The methodology's response at §4.5.5 (treat positive detection as a strong signal, treat negative detection as inconclusive) reflects this dynamic.

Reverse image search as a forensic technique is community practice rather than academic literature; the Bellingcat investigator's guide and Bazzell's OSINT references document the practitioner techniques. Specific platforms (Google reverse image search, Yandex, TinEye, Bing) have different strengths; the standard practitioner technique is to use multiple platforms because they index different sources.

## 2.8 Networked privacy and pseudonymity scholarship

A body of scholarship on networked privacy and pseudonymity is directly relevant to the methodology's ethical posture, particularly to the audience exclusions in §1.2 and §10.2. This subsection surveys the works that establish pseudonymity as a legitimate privacy practice and that document the populations for whom pseudonymity functions as a safety mechanism.

Nissenbaum's 2009 book "Privacy in Context" establishes the contextual integrity framework, which treats privacy as the appropriate flow of information within the norms of a given context rather than as a binary public-private distinction. The framework is directly relevant to the methodology's treatment of pseudonymous accounts: a person using one account in one context and another in another context is exercising contextual integrity, not engaging in inauthentic behavior, and the methodology's exclusion of signals that primarily detect legitimate pseudonymity (§4.8.2) is grounded in the contextual-integrity framing.

danah boyd's work on networked privacy, particularly the 2014 book "It's Complicated: The Social Lives of Networked Teens," documents how younger users construct privacy through obscurity, identity separation across platforms, and audience management rather than through technical access controls. boyd and Marwick's 2014 paper "Networked privacy: How teenagers negotiate context in social media" in New Media & Society develops the conceptual framework most directly relevant to the methodology's understanding of multi-account use as a legitimate privacy practice.

Marwick's broader work on networked privacy, including the 2018 edited volume "Privacy at the Margins" (with Fontaine and Lewis), documents how marginalized populations construct privacy under conditions that mainstream privacy frameworks do not address. The volume includes treatments of LGBTQ youth, sex workers, undocumented immigrants, and survivors of intimate-partner violence, each of which is among the populations the methodology's audience exclusions name as outside the methodology's scope.

Tufekci's 2017 book "Twitter and Tear Gas" provides context on the role of platform-mediated organization in social movements, with particular attention to how activists in authoritarian contexts use pseudonymity for both safety and effectiveness. The book documents specific cases (Turkish protests, Egyptian uprising, Hong Kong protests) where pseudonymous coordination was both legitimate and necessary, and where attribution methodologies would have caused direct harm if applied.

Phillips and Milner's 2017 book "The Ambivalent Internet" provides context on how online communities construct identity through complex relationships to authenticity, including the legitimate use of multiple accounts and personas for purposes that mainstream framings of authenticity do not accommodate.

Donovan's work at the Shorenstein Center and now at her own research center has developed the concept of "media manipulation" as a framework distinct from disinformation; her work documents how vulnerable populations are sometimes the targets of manipulation campaigns while also being users of pseudonymity for legitimate purposes, complicating any framework that treats pseudonymity as inherently suspect.

## 2.9 Threat modeling for vulnerable populations

A more recent academic literature develops threat models for specific vulnerable populations, with attention to how platform tooling and investigation methodology can produce harm even when applied with good intentions.

Matthews and colleagues' 2017 CHI paper "Stories from survivors: Privacy and security practices when coping with intimate partner abuse" is a foundational paper for threat modeling in the intimate-partner-abuse context. The paper documents the specific ways survivors construct privacy under conditions of ongoing threat from someone with deep knowledge of their identity, and is required reading for anyone considering applying attribution methodology in contexts where intimate-partner abuse may be relevant.

Lerner, Zeng, and Roesner's 2020 work on threat models for queer YouTube users develops the framework for thinking about how content-creator pseudonymity functions for queer users facing potential exposure. The work generalizes to broader queer-platform-use contexts and is recommended as background for understanding the LGBTQ exclusion category at §10.2.2.

McGregor and colleagues at the University of Washington have published on threat models for journalists working with sources, particularly in contexts where source protection requires the journalist's own pseudonymity. The journalism-source threat model overlaps with the methodology's protections for journalists' sources in §10.2.4.

The broader literature on usable security for vulnerable populations (Wuyts and colleagues on threat modeling methodology, the various USENIX SOUPS conference papers on populations-specific threat modeling) provides the methodological frame for thinking about how investigation methodologies can produce harm against populations the methodology does not intend to serve.

The methodology's audience exclusions in §1.2 and §10.2 are partly informed by this body of work. The exclusions are not novel; they reflect what the vulnerable-populations threat-modeling literature has established about which populations require structural protection against attribution methodology.

## 2.10 Legal and ethical context

The legal context for scraping public platform data has been shaped substantially by the *hiQ Labs v. LinkedIn* litigation in the United States federal courts (2017 through 2022). The Ninth Circuit's rulings in this case narrowed the application of the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) to scraping of public profiles, holding that public data scraping does not constitute unauthorized access for CFAA purposes. The Supreme Court's 2021 decision in *Van Buren v. United States*, while not directly about scraping, narrowed CFAA interpretation more broadly and is relevant context. The legal context outside the United States varies substantially by jurisdiction; the European Union's GDPR creates obligations around personal data that affect collection methodology even when the data is technically public, and various jurisdictions have specific laws on data scraping that practitioners should consult counsel about.

The ethical context for online harassment has been developed substantially in the legal-academic literature. Citron's 2014 book "Hate Crimes in Cyberspace" is a foundational academic treatment of online harassment as a phenomenon distinct from offline harassment, with particular attention to the gendered patterns of online abuse. Citron's subsequent work, including the 2022 book "The Fight for Privacy: Protecting Dignity, Identity, and Love in the Digital Age," extends the framework to broader questions of privacy as a civil right. Citron's collaboration with Franks has produced specific legal-policy work on the regulation of nonconsensual intimate imagery and related harms.

Phillips's 2015 book "This Is Why We Can't Have Nice Things" provides cultural context on troll communities and harassment culture, which is useful background for understanding the social dynamics in which sockpuppet networks operate. Phillips's collaboration with Milner (cited above) extends the cultural-studies framing to the broader ambivalence-of-internet question.

The media-manipulation context relevant to coordinated inauthentic behavior has been addressed in practitioner-oriented academic work. Marwick and Lewis's 2017 Data and Society report "Media Manipulation and Disinformation Online" is the foundational practitioner-academic document and is recommended as the entry point to the field. Subsequent Data and Society reports extend the framework to specific cases and populations.

The platform-policy context for coordinated inauthentic behavior has been substantially shaped by Meta's 2018 framing of the concept and subsequent operational definitions. Meta's quarterly Coordinated Inauthentic Behavior Reports, Twitter's transparency reports during the API-open period, Google's threat-analysis-group publications, and the equivalent documents from other major platforms provide operational context for understanding what platforms consider in scope for their own enforcement. The platform-side documentation is uneven in detail and reliability but provides a baseline for what the methodology's intended audience would compare against.

The Berkman Klein Center for Internet and Society at Harvard, the Stanford Internet Observatory, the Oxford Internet Institute's Computational Propaganda Project (now Programme on Democracy and Technology), and the Shorenstein Center on Media, Politics, and Public Policy at Harvard Kennedy School are the major academic centers producing work in this space. Their publications and ongoing research programs are recommended as resources for practitioners and researchers using the methodology.

## 2.11 What this methodology contributes

The methodology contributes three things that the surveyed prior work does not, in combination, provide.

**A documented practitioner methodology with explicit commitments.** OSINT practitioner methodology (§2.5) is documented but generally lacks the chain-of-custody and confidence-calibration commitments the methodology specifies. Academic authorship-attribution methodology (§2.4) has explicit commitments but does not address the broader signal taxonomy the methodology requires. Coordinated-behavior detection at the network level (§2.3) addresses platform-scale coordination but not the cluster-level attribution problem at the scale individual practitioners face. Commercial tooling (§2.6) operates under undocumented methodology that supports neither external verification nor replication. The methodology fills the gap of a documented methodology with explicit commitments that practitioners can adhere to and that reviewers can evaluate against.

**Accessibility to practitioners outside well-resourced institutions.** The commercial tooling (§2.6) is inaccessible to individual practitioners, small newsrooms, and most academic researchers. The network-level academic methodology (§2.3) typically requires platform partnership or substantial paid API access. The methodology presented here is designed to be applicable on infrastructure that the methodology's intended practitioners can afford, using scraping techniques (§5.3) that work in the post-API-paywall environment.

**A reference implementation alongside the methodology.** Many methodology papers in this space publish methodology without working implementation, leaving practitioners to build their own implementations and to converge or diverge from the published methodology in implementation-specific ways. The methodology is published alongside a reference implementation under AGPL-3.0, which gives practitioners a working starting point and gives reviewers a concrete artifact to evaluate the methodology against. The reference implementation is one realization of the methodology, not the only valid one, and the methodology is platform-agnostic in architecture despite the reference implementation being Cloudflare-pinned.

The combination of these three contributions defines the methodology's position. The methodology does not claim to do something none of the prior work does at any individual layer; it claims to combine commitments, accessibility, and reference implementation in a form that the prior work has not provided together. Practitioners with access to the prior work's individual contributions (paid OSINT tooling, platform partnerships, academic resources) may prefer those over the methodology presented here. Practitioners without access to the prior work's individual contributions have a methodology that meets standards similar to the existing alternatives without requiring the resources that the existing alternatives demand.

The methodology is also positioned in relation to the networked-privacy and threat-modeling literatures (§2.8 and §2.9). Where those literatures have established that pseudonymity is a legitimate privacy practice for many populations and that attribution methodology can produce harm against those populations, the methodology takes those findings as constraints rather than as obstacles. The audience exclusions in §1.2, the deliberately excluded signal categories in §4.8, and the ethical considerations developed in §10 reflect the methodology's acceptance of these constraints as part of what makes the methodology defensible.
