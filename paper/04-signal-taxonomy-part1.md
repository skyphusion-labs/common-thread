# 4. Signal Taxonomy

This section enumerates the signals that the methodology uses to characterize and compare accounts. Each signal is presented with the same structure: a definition of what it observes, what it can detect, what it cannot detect, its known failure modes in both directions, the extraction method used in the reference implementation, and citations to relevant prior work.

No single signal in this taxonomy carries attribution on its own. The methodology produces attribution claims by combining multiple signals; the confidence levels defined in §3.2 are calibrated against signal combinations, not individual signals. A practitioner who finds a strong signal in one category and treats it as sufficient evidence is misusing the methodology. The framework requires combinations across categories, and the reference implementation enforces this in the attribution reasoning layer.

The taxonomy is organized into eight categories. This section covers the first four: account metadata, temporal patterns, linguistic style, and network relationships. The remaining four (visual signals, crossplatform signals, metadata leakage, and deliberately excluded signals) are covered in §4.5 through §4.8.

The category boundaries are not crisp. Some signals could plausibly fit in more than one category; placement reflects where the signal is most useful as a starting point in an investigation, not a claim about its taxonomic essence. The reference implementation surfaces signals by category in its output, but the attribution reasoning layer is category-agnostic and considers all available signals together.

## 4.1 Account metadata signals

Account metadata signals are observable from the account profile alone, without watching any account activity. They are the cheapest signals to collect (a single profile fetch yields most of them) and the weakest in isolation. Their value is as filters and corroboration: metadata signals can rule accounts in or out of an investigation efficiently, and they can corroborate stronger signals from other categories.

### 4.1.1 Creation-date clustering

**What it detects.** Accounts created close together in time. When an operator builds a network, the accounts in that network often share a creation-date window. The window can be hours, days, or weeks; the signal weakens as the window widens.

**What it does not detect.** Legitimate cohort effects. Accounts created during a platform migration event, a viral signup moment, or a news-driven surge will share creation dates without any operator coordination. The signal is meaningful only against a background rate of account creation in the relevant community.

**False-positive modes.** Cohort effects are the dominant false-positive source. After Elon Musk's acquisition of Twitter, millions of accounts were created on Bluesky and Mastodon within compressed windows; treating that as a sockpuppet signal would be wrong. The same effect occurs at smaller scale around news events, platform feature launches, and academic class assignments that require students to create accounts.

**False-negative modes.** Operators who let accounts age before deploying them. A patient operator can create accounts months or years before activating them, defeating creation-date clustering entirely. The signal is most useful against unsophisticated networks; sophisticated networks will not produce it.

**Extraction.** Parse account creation timestamp from profile metadata. Compute pairwise time deltas across the account set. Compare against the base rate of account creation in the relevant community over the same window, sourced from a control corpus.

### 4.1.2 Display name and handle patterns

**What it detects.** Naming conventions that follow templates. Display names with similar structure (first-last with numeric suffix, similar capitalization, similar use of emoji or punctuation), handles that follow shared patterns, bios that match templates.

**What it does not detect.** Thoughtful operators who deliberately vary naming across personas. The signal targets lazy network building, not careful network building.

**False-positive modes.** Platform-suggested defaults (the autogenerated handles platforms offer when a preferred handle is taken), niche communities with naming conventions (gaming clans, language-learning subcultures), generational naming patterns within a demographic cohort.

**False-negative modes.** Deliberate variation by the operator. An operator who reads this paper will vary naming across personas as a baseline countermeasure.

**Extraction.** Compute pairwise edit distance across display names, bios, and handle strings. Tokenize bios and apply n-gram similarity. Flag accounts whose distance to at least one other account in the seed set is below a community-relative threshold.

### 4.1.3 Bio template matching

**What it detects.** Bios assembled from a recognizable template. Sockpuppet operators sometimes rely on a template ("name, location, hobby, pet, political identifier") that is detectable even when specific contents vary.

**Extraction.** Parse bios into typed slots (location string, occupation, identifiers). Compare slot patterns across accounts. A network that consistently fills the same slots in the same order is more template-bound than a community that fills slots organically.

This signal is closely related to §4.1.2 and is often surfaced together with it.

### 4.1.4 Verification status

**What it detects.** Coordinated purchase of platform verification badges. After verification was decoupled from identity verification on most major platforms, the badge became a paid signal. Accounts that purchased verification around the same date may be coordinated.

**What it does not detect.** Legitimate verification purchases. Many users purchase verification individually for legitimate reasons; clustering by purchase date is weak in isolation.

**Extraction.** Verification badge present or absent. Badge purchase date if available from the platform API or visible in account history.

This signal is most useful in combination with creation-date clustering: an account network that was both created and verified in nearby windows is a stronger signal than either alone.

### 4.1.5 Profile customization patterns

**What it detects.** Either uniform customization (matching banner schemes, similar pinned-post patterns) or uniform neglect (default avatar across multiple accounts, no banner, no pinned post). Operators sometimes signal-by-omission: the accounts they treat as throwaway personas may share a pattern of nonengagement with profile features.

**False-positive modes.** New users who haven't yet customized their profiles. Privacy-conscious users who deliberately minimize profile information.

**Extraction.** Enumerate profile fields. Score each account against the platform default. Cluster accounts by customization profile.

### 4.1.6 Stated location

**What it detects.** Geolocation claims in the location field. When a network shares an operator who lives in a specific region, persona accounts may either share a stated location (which is suspicious because it correlates) or carefully differentiate (which is suspicious because the differentiation pattern across multiple personas is itself a fingerprint).

**False-positive modes.** Real people in real locations. The signal is meaningful only against base rates.

**Extraction.** Extract location string. Geocode where possible. Compute pairwise similarity and compare against background rate for the community.

## 4.2 Temporal signals

Temporal signals are observable from when accounts post. They require a collection window long enough to surface patterns, typically several weeks for cadence signals and at least one triggering event for response-latency signals. Temporal signals are stronger than metadata signals in isolation and are often the most distinctive signals in a well-resourced investigation, because the operator's underlying schedule is difficult to mask while maintaining account utility.

### 4.2.1 Posting cadence correlation

**What it detects.** Accounts that post at similar times of day and days of week, with similar overall frequency. An operator running multiple accounts will tend to post on all of them during the operator's active hours, even when the operator tries to space the posts to avoid simultaneous activity.

**What it does not detect.** Operators who post on a deliberate schedule using scheduling tools, especially when those tools are configured differently across accounts.

**False-positive modes.** Timezonebased clustering (everyone in a given region will share active hours), professional schedules (everyone with a 9-to-5 job is active in similar windows), news-cycle-driven activity (everyone is posting about an event when it breaks).

**False-negative modes.** Deliberate cadence randomization. Scheduled posting through automation tools. Operators who post from multiple devices in multiple timezones.

**Extraction.** Bin posts by hour-of-day and day-of-week. Compute the distribution per account. Use Jensen-Shannon divergence or cosine similarity to compare distributions pairwise. Normalize against the background rate of activity in the relevant community over the same window.

### 4.2.2 Response latency to triggering events

This is one of the strongest signals in the taxonomy when applicable. It requires identifying triggering events: moments when the operator has reason to act on multiple accounts in rapid sequence.

**What it detects.** Accounts that respond to specific triggering events with characteristic latency distributions. A sockpuppet network responding to a triggering event will produce a latency distribution that is distinct from the latency distribution of organic readers (who respond in hours or days) and from the latency distribution of automated bot networks (which respond in milliseconds). The sockpuppet latency band is recognizable: typically minutes to tens of minutes, reflecting the operator switching between accounts and composing or selecting responses.

**What it does not detect.** Single-event coincidences. The signal requires multiple triggering events to be meaningful. A single 41-minute response from two accounts could be coincidence; ten such responses across ten events is not.

**False-positive modes.** Users with push notifications enabled for the triggering account, who all see the trigger at the same time and respond at similar speeds. Niche communities with established norms of quick response.

**False-negative modes.** Operators who randomize response delay. Operators who deliberately delay responses on some accounts.

**Extraction.** Identify triggering events in the collection corpus (filings served, opposing-party tweets, news events relevant to the network). For each event and each account in the seed set, measure the delta between event timestamp and the account's first response or amplification. Build a per-account latency distribution. Compare pairwise.

Practitioners should be conservative about which events count as triggering. The methodology requires the trigger to be public and observable; private triggers (DMs the operator received) produce response latencies that look organic and are not useful for this signal.

### 4.2.3 Active-hour distribution

**What it detects.** The hours during which each account is active, and the hours during which it is silent. Operators have to sleep, eat, and work. Accounts run by the same operator have correlated active periods.

**What it does not detect.** Operators who use scheduled posting to fill periods when they are not actually present. Operators with atypical schedules (shift workers, parents of infants, insomniacs).

**False-positive modes.** Timezone clustering. Professional schedule clustering.

**False-negative modes.** Scheduled posting through automation. Operators with multiple shifts (a network run by two people in different timezones may produce a 24-hour active distribution that defeats the signal).

**Extraction.** Hour-of-day frequency distribution per account. Cluster accounts whose distributions are highly correlated.

### 4.2.4 Quiet-period correlation

**What it detects.** Simultaneous gaps in activity across accounts. Operators take vacations, get sick, lose internet access. Coordinated accounts go quiet together for the same reasons.

**What it does not detect.** Quiet periods driven by platform-wide effects (outages, holidays where most users are away, news cycles where many users are silent). These produce correlated silence across many accounts in the community, not specifically the seed set.

**False-positive modes.** As noted, platformwide silence events.

**False-negative modes.** Operators who maintain posting during their offline periods using scheduled posts.

**Extraction.** Identify silence periods longer than a threshold (24 hours, 72 hours, one week) per account. For each pair of accounts, compute the fraction of silence periods that overlap. Compare against the community base rate.

### 4.2.5 Burst correlation

**What it detects.** Accounts that increase activity together. Different from response-latency: response latency measures how fast an account responds to a specific event; burst correlation measures whether multiple accounts increase posting frequency around the same time.

**Extraction.** For each account, identify burst periods (windows in which posting frequency exceeds the account's baseline by a configurable threshold, typically 2-3 standard deviations). For each pair of accounts, measure overlap of burst periods.

## 4.3 Linguistic signals

Linguistic signals are observable from what accounts say and how they say it. This category has the deepest prior literature, dating to authorship attribution work that predates modern computing. The signals are stronger than metadata and competitive with temporal in well-resourced investigations, and they are robust to most casual countermeasures.

**Terminology.** The taxonomy uses *linguistic* for this category. Subsections such as §4.3.1 use *stylometric* for the specific authorship-attribution markers drawn from the stylometrics literature. The reference implementation records all §4.3 features under the signal-table category name `stylometric` (see §6.2.3 and §7.3.1). The two names refer to the same category; practitioners comparing paper text to implementation output should treat `stylometric` as the implementation label for §4.3 linguistic signals.

The prior literature is critical here. Authorship attribution as a field is mature, with established methods, known failure modes, and a substantial empirical record. Practitioners should read at least Burrows (2002) on Delta, Koppel et al. (2009) on the general authorship attribution problem, and Stamatatos (2009) on the survey of methods. For adversarial-context work, Brennan and Greenstadt (2009) and the follow-on stylometric defense literature is essential reading; it describes how a writer can deliberately obscure stylometric signal, which is exactly what a sophisticated sockpuppet operator will attempt.

### 4.3.1 Stylometric markers

**What it detects.** The authorial fingerprint visible in writing style. Specific markers include sentence-length distribution, function-word frequency, punctuation habits, capitalization patterns, and characteristic transitions.

Function-word frequency is the workhorse of stylometric attribution. The most frequent words in a language ("the," "of," "and," "to," "a," "in," "is," "it," "you," "that") carry strong authorship signal precisely because writers use them unconsciously. A writer's distribution over function words is stable across topics and is difficult to consciously alter without making the writing read as stilted.

Punctuation and capitalization carry similar signal at smaller scale. Whether a writer uses the Oxford comma, how they punctuate the end of an informal sentence, how they capitalize hashtags or proper nouns, whether they use sentence-initial capitalization at all in casual writing: these are stable habits that bleed across personas.

**What it does not detect.** Writing that has been heavily edited, AI-rewritten, or deliberately obfuscated using stylometric defense techniques.

**False-positive modes.** Stylebook adherence. A writer who follows a publication's stylebook will share function-word and punctuation patterns with other writers at the same publication. Educational backgrounds produce similar effects: writers trained at the same program, in the same language, at the same level of formality, will share more style than the population average.

**False-negative modes.** AI-assisted writing has become a substantial false-negative source. A writer who runs all output through an LLM produces text that reads more like the LLM than like the writer. Stylometric defense tools (Anonymouth and its successors) are designed specifically to flatten stylometric signal.

**Extraction.** Compute a feature vector per account: function-word frequencies over the top 100 to 500 most common words, sentence-length distribution, punctuation rates, capitalization rates. Use Burrows' Delta or a similar normalized distance metric to compare accounts pairwise. Filter out accounts with too little text to support reliable measurement; the minimum corpus size varies by language but is typically several thousand words.

### 4.3.2 Topic vocabulary overlap

**What it detects.** Shared specialized vocabulary across accounts. When two personas claim different identities but share niche vocabulary (technical jargon, in-group slang, specialized references), the shared vocabulary is a signal.

**What it does not detect.** Members of the same legitimate community will share niche vocabulary. The signal is meaningful only against a community baseline.

**False-positive modes.** Legitimate community membership.

**False-negative modes.** Operators who carefully avoid shared vocabulary when posting from different personas.

**Extraction.** Compute TF-IDF per account against a background corpus from the broader community. Identify each account's distinctive vocabulary (highest TF-IDF terms). Compute pairwise overlap on the top N distinctive terms, weighted by rarity.

### 4.3.3 Idiosyncratic phrase reuse

**What it detects.** Verbatim or nearverbatim reuse of distinctive phrases across accounts. Writers have pet phrases, rhetorical tics, characteristic transitions. When these phrases appear across multiple accounts, the signal is strong, because phrases are larger and rarer than individual words.

**What it does not detect.** Memes, viral phrases, and shared community vocabulary. The signal weakens as the phrase becomes more common.

**False-positive modes.** Memes are the dominant false-positive source. A phrase that goes viral will appear across thousands of accounts in a short window.

**False-negative modes.** Paraphrase. A careful operator who varies phrasing across personas defeats this signal.

**Extraction.** Compute n-grams (typically 3-grams through 7-grams) per account. Weight by novelty against a background corpus from the broader community. Identify n-grams that are rare in the background and appear in multiple seed accounts. Surface the most distinctive shared n-grams for review.

### 4.3.4 Code-switching patterns

**What it detects.** Patterns of switching between languages, registers, or formality levels. An operator who naturally code-switches between two languages may leak the same code-switching pattern across personas that are supposed to be monolingual. An operator whose natural register is informal may produce occasional informalisms in a persona that claims to be formal.

**What it does not detect.** Operators who maintain register discipline strictly. Operators who genuinely speak only one language.

**Extraction.** Per-post classification of register (formal, neutral, informal) and language. Sequential analysis of register switches. Compute per-account register distribution and switching frequency.

### 4.3.5 Typo and error patterns

**What it detects.** Recurring typos, grammatical errors, and autocorrect signatures. These are motorlevel and habit-level fingerprints, harder to consciously alter than higher-level style choices.

**What it does not detect.** Heavily edited text. AI-rewritten text. Text that has been passed through a grammar checker.

**False-positive modes.** Shared autocorrect dictionaries. Shared L1 transfer effects (writers whose first language is the same will make similar errors when writing in their second language).

**False-negative modes.** Editorial cleanup. Modern AI writing assistants flatten this signal substantially.

**Extraction.** Build an error taxonomy (their/there/they're, then/than, apostrophe errors, common autocorrect substitutions). Compute per-account error rates. Compare pairwise.

## 4.4 Network signals

Network signals are observable from accounttoaccount relationships: who follows whom, who engages with whose content, who appears in whose mentions. They require collection that extends beyond individual account timelines to include follower lists and engagement records.

Network signals have a structural strength that the previous categories lack: they describe relationships that an operator cannot fully control unilaterally. An operator can choose what to post, when to post, and how to write, but they cannot fully choose who follows their accounts or who engages with their content. This makes some network signals robust to countermeasures that defeat other categories.

The prior literature on coordinated inauthentic behavior detection (see Pacheco et al. 2020 and the related body of work on network analysis of disinformation) is the relevant background.

### 4.4.1 Follower overlap

**What it detects.** Accounts whose follower sets have abnormally high overlap. Two accounts in the same operator's network often share a follower base, especially in early life (followers acquired by the operator's mutuals or by amplification from other accounts in the network).

**What it does not detect.** Accounts in the same niche community without operator coordination. The signal requires normalization against community baseline; raw follower overlap is meaningless without context.

**False-positive modes.** Niche-community membership is the dominant false-positive source. Two accounts that post about the same specialized topic will share followers without any operator coordination. Follower-purchase services create artificial overlap in patterns that may resemble sockpuppet networks but are not.

**False-negative modes.** Operators who deliberately seed each persona with a distinct follower base. Operators who use older accounts with established follower bases unrelated to the network.

**Extraction.** Collect follower lists per account in the seed set. Compute Jaccard similarity pairwise. Normalize against community baseline computed from a control corpus of accounts in the same niche.

### 4.4.2 Mutual-follow patterns

**What it detects.** Accounts that follow each other early in their respective lifetimes, especially when both are new. Networks tend to form mutual-follow handshakes at network formation, particularly when the operator wants the accounts to amplify each other from the start.

**What it does not detect.** Mutual follows established over long time horizons between accounts in legitimate communities.

**False-positive modes.** Reciprocal-follow communities (some communities have norms of returning follows; mutual follows are routine).

**False-negative modes.** Operators who deliberately avoid mutual following between their own accounts.

**Extraction.** For each pair of accounts in the seed set, determine whether they mutually follow. If so, determine the timestamp of the second follow (the one that closed the mutual). Compare against account-creation timestamps.

### 4.4.3 Coengagement timing on thirdparty content

**What it detects.** Multiple accounts engaging with the same third-party content within a short window. Stronger than just "both engaged"; the timing matters. An operator who runs multiple accounts will tend to engage from all of them in close succession, because they are looking at the same content in the same browser session.

**What it does not detect.** Largeamplification cascades where many accounts engage near-simultaneously as a content piece propagates. The signal requires the engaging accounts to be a small set; broad cascades are different phenomena.

**False-positive modes.** Push-notification-driven engagement (followers who all see content at the same time).

**False-negative modes.** Deliberate desynchronization. Use of different devices for different accounts to break browser-session correlation.

**Extraction.** For each third-party post engaged by two or more accounts in the seed set, measure the time delta between engagements. Build a per-pair distribution of co-engagement deltas. Compare against the platform's broader distribution.

### 4.4.4 Crossaccount amplification

**What it detects.** Disproportionate amplification of one account's content by other accounts in the seed set. Distinctive when the amplified account is new or has low organic following: amplification by a consistent set of other accounts in the network is a signal that the amplifying accounts are coordinated.

**What it does not detect.** Amplification by accounts outside the seed set, which the methodology does not have visibility into.

**False-positive modes.** Friend networks where amplification is genuine but not coordinated in the sockpuppet sense.

**False-negative modes.** Amplification through accounts not in the seed set. Operators who deliberately route amplification through indirect paths.

**Extraction.** For each account in the seed set, identify its top amplifiers (other accounts that have engaged with its content most frequently). Cross-reference with seed-set membership. Compute the fraction of an account's amplification that comes from inside the seed set, normalized against baseline.

### 4.4.5 Block and mute coordination

**What it detects.** Coordinated blocking or muting patterns across accounts. When visible (some platforms expose block lists or muted-account lists), shared block lists are a strong signal of operator coordination.

**Practical limit.** This signal is rarely available. Most platforms do not expose blocking or muting lists. Tools that previously provided this visibility on Twitter have been deprecated or are no longer reliable. The signal is listed for completeness but is operationally weak in the current environment.

**Extraction.** Where platform APIs or public lists expose blocking or muting data, collect per-account block and mute sets. Compute Jaccard similarity pairwise.

## Combining signals from §4.1-§4.4

The four categories covered here produce signals at different cost-to-strength ratios. Metadata signals are cheap and weak. Temporal signals are moderately expensive and strong when triggering events are available. Linguistic signals are expensive in corpus requirements but strong and robust. Network signals are expensive to collect and structurally distinctive.

A well-formed investigation will collect signals from at least three of the four categories before producing an attribution claim. Attribution claims that rest on signals from only one category are vulnerable to the failure modes of that category; signals from multiple categories combine to reach the confidence thresholds defined in §3.2.

The next sections (§4.5 through §4.8) cover the remaining signal categories: visual signals, crossplatform signals, metadata leakage, and the signals that the methodology deliberately excludes. The structure is the same: definition, what it detects, what it does not detect, failure modes, extraction method.
